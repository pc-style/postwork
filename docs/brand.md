# Brand Identity Document

Compiled by Draper - 13/07/2026

How to use this document

This is Postwork's brand guide. It is written for anyone who makes something in Postwork's name: copy, UI, marketing, social, or code. Use the relevant section whenever you make something. If you are an LLM, treat every section as a hard constraint, not a suggestion.

---

## 1. Name

Postwork - from "post", the primary unit of communication in the product. A post is a durable, structured record: it has an author, a topic, a status, and a history. The name is also a quiet statement about what comes after the way work communication currently operates.

It is a product name and a position simultaneously. The thing you send is a post. The tool is Postwork. The two are inseparable.

---

## 2. Purpose

Work communication got optimised for interruption. Tools built around the live feed made every message feel equally urgent, buried decisions under conversation, and turned coming back to work into an archaeology project. Postwork exists because the communication system should never create more work than the work itself.

This purpose is used internally as the north star for product and brand decisions. When evaluating whether a feature, a campaign, or a line of copy belongs in Postwork, the test is simple: does it reduce the overhead of communication, or does it add to it? It also appears in longer-form external contexts - an About page, a company manifesto, an investor narrative - wherever Postwork needs to explain not just what it does but why it had to exist.

---

## 3. Positioning

Work communication that stays.

"Stays" is the entire product thesis in one word. Decisions don't disappear. Context survives the conversation. The team can leave for hours or days and come back to work that still makes sense. The line is plain and complete - it requires no contrast structure, no competitor reference, no explanation. It works on a website hero, an app store listing, a deck cover, or the opening line of a cold email. It is Postwork's most public-facing statement and should be the first thing a new user reads.

## 4. Brand Values

Brand values are the working rules behind every Postwork decision - what to build, what to say, and what to leave out. They are not aspirational. They are constraints. If a piece of copy, a product feature, or a marketing activity conflicts with these values, it does not belong in Postwork.

### Value 1 - Discipline

Postwork believes urgency should be rare enough to mean something. The product enforces this by design - not by asking teams to change their behaviour, but by making low-context, high-frequency communication the awkward path. Discipline means protecting the signal by controlling the noise.

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Keep urgency scarce - cap it, surface the review, make it deliberate | Use notification language, unread counts, or presence indicators |
| Make the important post findable without asking the team to stay online | Reward the most active communicators with visibility |
| Apply the same constraint to brand communications - say less, mean more | Publish content, updates, or copy that doesn't earn its place |

### Value 2 - Durability

Postwork believes a decision made today should be as findable in three months as it is in three minutes. The product is built around the post as infrastructure - a record with a clear author, a topic, a status, and a history. Durability means the work survives the conversation.

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Design for the post that needs to be found later, not the message that needs to be seen now | Optimise for real-time engagement or activity metrics |
| Use AI to make context recoverable, not to generate more content | Add features that create more communication overhead |
| Write copy that is specific and referenceable - something you could point to | Use language so general it could belong to any product |

### Value 3 - Directness

Postwork believes every claim should be concrete enough to prove in the product. The brand does not use soft promises, transformation language, or adjectives that aren't earned. It says what the product does, names the mechanism, and trusts the reader to draw the conclusion.

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Name the feature, the outcome, and the user in specific terms | Use abstract claims like "supercharge", "unlock", or "empower" |
| Let the product speak - the copy describes what it does, not how it feels | Add emotional language that the product hasn't earned yet |
| Be plain when plain is more accurate than polished | Polish a sentence until it loses its meaning |

## 5. Tone of Voice

The Precise Builder.

Postwork writes like the people who build and use it - founders, engineers, product leads who think clearly about systems and notice when copy is vague. Sentences are short and complete. Mechanisms are named, not gestured at. The tone is confident without being arrogant, direct without being blunt. Dry wit is allowed when it is earned. Enthusiasm is not performed. The reader is always treated as a peer - someone who already knows the problem and is evaluating whether the product actually solves it.

It is not documentation, but it shares documentation's discipline: precision over flair, structure over momentum, finished over provisional.

### Tone Do's & Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Name the mechanism - how does the product actually work? | Use metaphors that obscure what the product does |
| Write for someone who reads carefully | Write for someone who skims app store descriptions |
| Trust silence - not every sentence needs a follow-up | Pad copy with transitions and affirmations |
| Sound like you built it | Sound like you hired someone to describe it |
| Let each sentence stand alone and mean something | Write sentences that need context to land |
| Use dry wit when the moment calls for it | Use emoji, exclamation marks, or fake friendliness |

### Tone Examples

| Don't write this | Write this instead |
| --- | --- |
| "Postwork supercharges your team's productivity with AI-powered collaboration!" | "Your team's decisions stay findable. Even after a week away." |
| "Unlock seamless async communication across your entire organisation." | "Posts don't disappear. The context is still there when you get back." |
| "We're reimagining what it means to communicate at work 🚀" | "We built the tool we wanted. It keeps important work visible." |
| "Collaborate effortlessly with your team in real time." | "Each post holds its thread, its history, and its status. Nothing gets buried." |
| "AI-powered insights to keep your team aligned." | "When you come back, the summary tells you what changed." |

## 6. Counter-positioning

Slack, Teams, Basecamp, and Twist share one assumption: communication is a stream, ordered by time, where the most recent message is the most visible. They differ in pace, polish, and price. But none of them has committed to the durable post as the primary unit. That position is open, and Postwork owns it.

Postwork does not name competitors in external copy. It names the model - the stream - and positions itself as the alternative. The reader draws the comparison themselves.

### The Counter-Positions Postwork Owns

Position 1 - The Durable Record

"Most team communication tools are built for talking. Postwork is built for knowing."

The stream optimises for the live conversation. It is poor infrastructure for decisions that need to survive past the same afternoon. Every competitor has bolted AI summaries or search onto a chat stream. Postwork starts with the post - the record - and builds the conversation around it.

Position 2 - Urgency That Means Something

"When everything is urgent, nothing is. Postwork keeps urgency scarce by design."

Every stream-based tool treats a new message and a critical decision with identical visual weight. Postwork's feed ranks by urgency, not recency, and caps urgent posts so the signal stays clean. Teams that use urgency deliberately communicate more clearly than teams that use it constantly.

Position 3 - The Tool That Lets You Leave

"Slack is a good tool for quick back-and-forth. It is a poor tool for finding a decision made three weeks ago. Postwork is built for the latter."

Postwork does not compete for attention. It does not have unread badge counts designed to draw teams back in. The product is useful precisely because you can leave it - and the work is still there, intact, when you return.

### In Practice

Use these positions in website copy, product descriptions, and talks - but never in a way that names a competitor directly. The framing is always "the stream model" or "most communication tools", not "Slack does X badly." The reader's own experience fills the gap. Avoid any language that sounds like a startup taking a swing at an incumbent - the tone should be matter-of-fact, not combative.

## 7. Golden Circle

The Golden Circle gives Postwork three levels of description it can use across every context - a website hero, an investor slide, an onboarding email, an elevator pitch. The Why is the belief behind the product. The How is the mechanism. The What is the product, plainly stated. Use whichever level fits the surface.

### Why

Work communication got built for interruption. Every tool optimised for the live feed - making everything feel urgent, burying decisions under conversation, and turning context into something you reconstruct rather than find. Teams end up spending as much time managing communication as doing the work it was meant to support. The communication system should never create more work than the work itself.

### How

Postwork makes the post - not the message - the primary unit of communication. Every post has a clear author, a topic, a status, and a history that survives the conversation. The feed ranks by urgency, not recency. AI makes context recoverable for anyone returning to a thread. The product makes low-context, high-frequency communication the awkward path - not by policy, but by design.

### What

A structured async communication tool for small technical teams. Posts that stay findable. Feeds that surface what matters. Context that survives the conversation.

## 8. Brand Inspirations

The brands whose DNA Postwork draws from. Each entry explains what to take and where to apply it.

### Linear

What it is: A project management tool for software teams, known for its opinionated design, speed, and disciplined product decisions. What to take: Linear's confidence in making defaults for users rather than asking users to configure everything. The product has a point of view and enforces it. Linear also demonstrates that a tool aimed at technical teams can have genuine visual and product craft - it does not have to choose between precision and beauty. The discipline to remove features that don't belong is as important as the discipline to build the ones that do. Where to apply it: Product design and copy - every default in Postwork should be a considered position, not a neutral starting point. When writing about product decisions, sound like a team that thought it through, not one that consulted everyone. Search stem: Linear app design

### Stripe

What it is: A payments infrastructure company for developers, known for documentation and marketing that explains technical products with unusual clarity and precision. What to take: Stripe's writing treats the reader as technically literate and rewards them for it. It explains what the product does, names the mechanism, and trusts the reader to draw the conclusion. It does not oversell. It does not soften technical language into vague promises. The Stripe Docs voice and the Stripe marketing voice are the same voice - precise, complete, and confident. Where to apply it: All written copy - website, product UI strings, documentation, onboarding, error messages. The standard is: could this sentence appear in a Stripe doc without feeling out of place? Search stem: Stripe developer writing

### GitHub

What it is: A code hosting and collaboration platform that became the default infrastructure layer for how software teams work. What to take: GitHub did not win by being loud. It won by becoming indispensable - the place where the actual work lived. The brand lesson is long-term positioning as infrastructure: a tool you depend on, not a product you evangelize. GitHub also demonstrates that a tool can have deep community trust without performing friendliness or over-communicating its own value. Where to apply it: Long-term brand positioning and go-to-market narrative. Postwork is building toward the same kind of trust - the place where team decisions live. The marketing should reflect that quiet confidence, not a startup urgency. Search stem: GitHub infrastructure brand

### Teenage Engineering

What it is: A Swedish electronics and design company known for products with a strong, recognisable visual identity built from restraint rather than decoration. What to take: Teenage Engineering's visual identity is immediately recognisable without relying on logo placement or brand coverage. The product is the brand. Every surface is considered. Nothing is there by accident or convention. The lesson for Postwork is that restraint is a design choice, not an absence of design - a warm near-black surface with precise typography and a single wine accent is more distinctive than a gradient and a rounded card. Where to apply it: Visual identity and UI direction. The product should look like it was made by people who care about small details. No generic SaaS components. No decoration that doesn't do work. Search stem: Teenage Engineering product design

## 9. Aesthetic Inspirations

For mood board generation, design briefs, and visual direction. Each reference is written with enough specificity to be passed directly to a visual tool, image generation prompt, or designer.

### Swiss Editorial Design (Graphic Design - Müller-Brockmann, Ruder, 1950s–70s)

The quality: Strong typographic hierarchy with deliberate white space. Type does the structural work - weight, size, and placement replace decorative elements entirely. The grid is visible in the result even when it is not shown. Colour is used sparingly and purposefully, never for decoration. The feeling is one of total control: nothing is there by accident, and nothing is missing. Extract for: Typography system and layout. Website hierarchy. Information density in the product UI. The principle is that spacing and type weight communicate structure more reliably than colour or iconography. Search stem: Swiss grid typography

### Archival Reading Rooms (Architecture/Interior - Bodleian, British Library reading rooms)

The quality: Warm, dark surfaces - deep wood, worn leather, aged paper - that feel like places designed for sustained attention rather than quick visits. Lighting is focused and low. The physical environment communicates that the information held here is worth preserving. There is no urgency in the space. Returning to it after time away feels like picking up where you left off, not catching up on what you missed. Extract for: Colour palette direction and dark mode UI. Hero imagery and brand photography mood. The warm near-black surfaces and wine or deep red accents come directly from this reference. The feeling of the product should echo the feeling of a space that holds things carefully. Search stem: Reading room dark interior

### Early Industrial Interfaces (Industrial Design/UI - Braun electrical controls, analogue broadcast equipment, 1960s–80s)

The quality: Direct, visible controls with clear state. Every dial, button, and indicator communicates exactly what it does and what state it is in. No abstraction, no animation to soften the interaction, no decoration around the functional elements. The interface respects the user's intelligence by showing them the system clearly. Colours are functional - red means active, grey means inactive - rather than expressive. Extract for: UI component design and interaction language. Status indicators, urgency signals, and post state should be direct and readable without requiring inference. The constraint is: if a control's state is not immediately visible, the design has failed. Applied to Postwork without retro cosplay - the principle, not the era. Search stem: Braun product controls

### Darkroom Photography (Photography - analogue darkroom process, 1950s–present)

The quality: Rich, warm blacks that are not flat - they hold depth and texture. A single restrained accent in deep red or wine that reads as both technical and warm. High contrast without harshness. The emotional register is focused, deliberate, and slow - the opposite of a screen optimised for quick consumption. The process is about waiting for the image to appear, not capturing as many as possible. Extract for: Colour palette foundation. The warm near-black of the Postwork UI comes from this reference, not from generic dark mode conventions. The wine or deep red accent is the safelight - present, purposeful, and never overused. Brand photography, if used, should feel closer to a developed print than a digital capture. Search stem: Darkroom safelight photography
